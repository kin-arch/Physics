Physics Short Questions & Answers

Comprehensive Revision Guide

1. Differentiate between physical and non-physical quantities.
  • Physical quantities: Measurable and expressed with units (e.g., length in meter, mass in kilogram).
  • Non-physical quantities: Cannot be measured and have no units (e.g., love, honesty).
  • Physical quantities are used in scientific calculations; non-physical are abstract.
2. Differentiate between base and derived physical quantities with examples.
  • Base quantities: Fundamental quantities not derived from others (length (l) - meter, mass (m) - kilogram, time (t) - second).
  • Derived quantities: Obtained by combining base quantities (velocity (v) - m/s, force (F) - Newton, energy (E) - Joule).
  • Example: Force = mass × acceleration (derived).
3. Express numbers in scientific notation.
Scientific notation is written as a × 10n, where 1 ≤ a < 10.
  • 4500 = 4.5 × 103
  • 0.002 = 2 × 10-3
Used to simplify very large or very small numbers.
4. Convert 4000 nanograms into micrograms.
1 microgram = 1000 nanograms
4000 ng ÷ 1000 = 4 μg
5. Show that micro is 1000 times smaller than milli.
Milli = 10-3
Micro = 10-6
Difference: 10-6 ÷ 10-3 = 10-3
So, micro is 1000 times smaller than milli.
6. Convert 25 micrometers into kilometers.
1 μm = 10-6 m and 1 m = 10-3 km
25 μm = 25 × 10-6 × 10-3 km
= 25 × 10-9 km
= 2.5 × 10-8 km
7. Differentiate between scalar and vector quantities.
  • Scalar quantities: Have magnitude only (speed, mass).
  • Vector quantities: Have magnitude and direction (velocity, force).
  • Vectors require direction for complete description.
8. Justify that distance, speed, time, mass, energy, temperature are scalars.
These quantities only have magnitude and no direction. Example: 10 m distance does not specify direction. Therefore, they are scalar quantities.
9. Justify that displacement, force, velocity, etc., are vectors.
These quantities require both magnitude and direction. Example: Velocity = 20 m/s east. Without direction, value is incomplete → vector quantities.
10. Steps to represent a vector in coordinate system.
  • Draw x-axis and y-axis.
  • Choose suitable scale.
  • Mark origin (0,0).
  • Draw an arrow showing magnitude and direction.
  • Label the vector properly.
11. Differentiate between precision and accuracy.
  • Accuracy: How close a measured value is to the true value.
  • Precision: How close repeated measurements are to each other.
  • Example: If the true value is 10 cm:
    • 9.9, 10.1 → accurate
    • 8.0, 8.1 → precise but not accurate
12. Estimate your age in seconds and minutes.
Assume age = 16 years
  • Minutes: 16 × 365 × 24 × 60 ≈ 8,409,600 minutes
  • Seconds: 8,409,600 × 60 ≈ 504,576,000 seconds
13. Differentiate between mechanical and digital stopwatch.
  • Mechanical stopwatch: Uses gears; less accurate; human reading error possible.
  • Digital stopwatch: Electronic; more accurate; displays exact numbers.
  • Digital is faster and more reliable.
14. What base quantities are involved in force, pressure, power, and charge?
  • Force: mass, length, time
  • Pressure: mass, length, time
  • Power: mass, length, time
  • Charge: current, time
15. Is it possible to accelerate an object without speeding up or slowing down? Justify.
Yes. Acceleration depends on change in velocity, not just speed. If direction changes (like circular motion), velocity changes → acceleration exists. Example: Object moving in a circle at constant speed.
16. Differentiate between average and instantaneous speed.
  • Average speed: Total distance ÷ total time.
  • Instantaneous speed: Speed at a specific moment.
  • Example: Speedometer shows instantaneous speed.
17. Differentiate between uniform and non-uniform velocity.
  • Uniform velocity: Constant speed and direction.
  • Non-uniform velocity: Speed or direction changes.
  • Turning motion = non-uniform velocity.
18. Define acceleration & deceleration.
  • Acceleration: Rate of change of velocity with time.
  • Deceleration: Negative acceleration (velocity decreases).
19. Differentiate between uniform & non-uniform acceleration.
  • Uniform acceleration: Equal change in velocity in equal time intervals.
  • Non-uniform acceleration: Unequal change in velocity.
  • Example: Free fall = uniform acceleration.
20. Can direction of velocity and acceleration be opposite? Explain briefly.
Yes. When an object slows down, acceleration acts opposite to velocity. Example: A moving car applying brakes.
21. Differentiate between mass and weight.
  • Mass: Amount of matter; constant; measured in kg.
  • Weight: Force due to gravity; changes with location; measured in newtons.
  • Weight = mass × gravity
22. Differentiate between contact and non-contact forces.
  • Contact forces: Require physical contact (friction, tension).
  • Non-contact forces: Act at a distance (gravity, magnetic).
23. Differentiate between different types of forces.
  • Gravitational (weight): Attraction between masses.
  • Friction: Opposes motion between surfaces.
  • Drag/Air resistance: Opposes motion in fluid/air.
  • Tension: Force in stretched string/rope.
  • Electrostatic: Between charged objects.
  • Magnetic: Between magnets.
  • Thrust (driving force): Push/pull moving object forward.
24. State fundamental forces.
There are three fundamental forces (as per syllabus wording): Gravitational force, Strong nuclear force, and Electroweak force.
25. State limitations of Newton's laws of motion.
  • Not valid at very high speeds (near speed of light).
  • Not applicable at atomic/subatomic level.
  • Fails in strong gravitational fields.
26. State and apply Newton’s three laws.
  • First Law: Object remains at rest or uniform motion unless acted upon by an external force.
  • Second Law: F = ma
  • Third Law: Every action has an equal and opposite reaction.
27. Deduce Newton’s first law from second law.
From second law: F = ma. If F = 0 → a = 0. So velocity remains constant → object stays at rest or uniform motion. This is Newton’s first law.
28. Why are cricket gloves padded with foam?
Foam increases the time of impact, reducing the force. From the impulse concept: Force ↓ when time ↑. So it protects hands from injury.
29. State Newton’s second law in terms of acceleration. Write its mathematical form.
Acceleration is directly proportional to force and inversely proportional to mass.
a = F / m or F = ma
30. Explain motion of rocket/gun using Newton’s 3rd law and conservation of momentum.
Rocket/gun pushes gases/bullet backward. According to Newton’s 3rd law, an equal and opposite reaction pushes the rocket/gun forward. Total momentum before and after remains constant.
31. Explain relationship between Newton’s laws and conservation of momentum.
Newton’s second law states force equals rate of change of momentum. Newton’s third law says action and reaction are equal and opposite. When two bodies interact, forces cancel internally, so total momentum remains constant.
32. Define and calculate momentum, impulse and resultant force.
  • Momentum (p): p = mv
  • Impulse (J): J = Δp = F × t
  • Resultant force (F): F = Δp / t
33. Differentiate between rolling and sliding friction.
Rolling friction occurs when an object rolls over a surface and is smaller. Sliding friction occurs when an object slides over a surface and is larger. Rolling friction causes less resistance.
34. Briefly describe any three methods to reduce friction.
  • Use lubricants like oil or grease.
  • Smooth or polish surfaces.
  • Use wheels or ball bearings instead of sliding.
35. Write advantages and disadvantages of friction.
Advantages: Helps in walking, allows vehicles to move, helps in writing.
Disadvantages: Causes wear and tear, produces heat (energy loss), reduces efficiency of machines.
36. Differentiate between like and unlike parallel forces.
Like parallel forces act in the same direction. Unlike parallel forces act in opposite directions. Like forces add up, unlike forces subtract.
37. Compare center of mass and center of gravity.
Center of mass: point where total mass is concentrated. Center of gravity: point where weight acts. In uniform gravity, both points are the same.
38. How can the stability of an object be improved?
Lower its center of gravity, increase base area, and keep weight evenly distributed.
39. Why do ice skates effortlessly slide on ice, while your shoes cause skidding?
Ice skates have a thin blade that reduces friction and may create a thin water layer. Shoes have larger surface area, causing irregular friction and less control, leading to skidding.
40. How do communication satellites stay in geostationary orbit?
They move with a specific speed so that gravitational force provides the required centripetal force. This keeps them fixed relative to Earth.
41. How does centripetal force keep a car from skidding on a curve?
Centripetal force (provided by friction between tires and road) keeps the car moving in a circular path. If speed is too high, friction is insufficient and the car skids.
42. Why does the sky appear blue? What is GPS?
Sky appears blue due to scattering of sunlight; blue light scatters more. GPS (Global Positioning System) is a satellite-based system used to determine location on Earth.
43. Define principle of moments and its types.
Principle of moments: For equilibrium, clockwise moments = anticlockwise moments.
Clockwise moment: turning effect in clockwise direction. Anticlockwise: opposite direction.
44. Define and calculate spring constant.
Spring constant (k) is the stiffness of a spring. Formula: k = F / x, where F = force and x = extension.
45. Define Hooke’s law. Write applications and formula.
Hooke’s law: Extension is directly proportional to applied force within elastic limit. Formula: F = kx. Applications: springs, weighing machines, vehicle suspension.
46. How can a small lifter lift a heavier load?
Using hydraulic systems (Pascal’s principle), a small force applied over a small area produces a large force over a larger area.
47. Why do static fluids exert force perpendicular to the surface?
In static fluids, there is no shear stress. Pressure acts equally in all directions, so force is always perpendicular to the surface.
48. Define and calculate pressure and efficiency.
Pressure: P = F / A. Efficiency: (Output / Input) × 100%.
49. Describe how pressure varies with force and area.
Pressure increases with force and decreases with area. Example: a sharp knife cuts better because of a small area leading to high pressure.
50. Justify that the atmosphere exerts pressure.
Atmospheric pressure is proven by experiments like barometers and suction cups. Air has weight, and this weight exerts pressure on Earth’s surface.
51. Describe that atmospheric pressure decreases with altitude.
Atmospheric pressure is due to the weight of air above us. At higher altitudes, there is less air above, so pressure decreases.
52. Explain that changes in atmospheric pressure indicate weather changes.
A fall in pressure usually means bad weather (rain, storms). A rise in pressure indicates clear weather.
53. Differentiate between manometer and barometer.
Barometer: measures atmospheric pressure. Manometer: measures pressure of gases in closed systems.
54. Analyze the working of a liquid barometer and manometer.
Liquid barometer: uses mercury column; height shows pressure. Manometer: measures pressure difference using liquid columns.
55. How does the shape of a thumb pin help penetrate surfaces easily?
The pointed end has a very small area, producing high pressure with small force, so it penetrates easily.
56. State and apply the principle of conservation of energy.
Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only converted. Example: a falling object converts potential energy into kinetic energy.
57. Why perpetual energy machines do not work?
They violate conservation of energy and always lose energy due to friction and heat.
58. Differentiate between renewable and non-renewable energy.
Renewable: can be replenished (solar, wind). Non-renewable: limited and exhaustible (coal, oil).
59. How is useful energy obtained from fuels and geothermal?
Biofuels: burning organic matter. Fossil fuels: chemical energy to heat. Geothermal: heat from Earth to steam.
60. Define work & power. Also define their SI units.
Work: W = Fd (Joule). Power: P = W / t (Watt).
61. State that a system cannot have 100% efficiency.
Some energy is always lost as heat, sound, or friction, so efficiency is always less than 100%.
62. A perpetual engine has efficiency of 1. Why it will not work?
Efficiency of 1 (100%) is impossible due to energy losses.
63. How does increasing power impact energy consumption?
Higher power means more energy used per unit time. So energy consumption increases.
64. A car has kinetic energy Ek. What happens if velocity is doubled?
KE ∝ v2. If velocity doubles, KE becomes 4 times.
65. A car moves at constant speed on a straight road. Is work done?
No net work is done because there is no change in kinetic energy and forces are balanced.
66. Explain that energy may be stored in different forms.
Energy can be stored as: Gravitational potential, Chemical, Elastic, Nuclear, Electrostatic, and Internal/thermal energy.
67. Relationship between internal energy and temperature.
Internal energy increases with temperature because particles move faster and have more kinetic energy.
68. Describe plasma as the fourth state of matter.
Plasma is an ionized gas with free electrons and ions. It conducts electricity and is found in stars.
69. Relationship between motion of particles and temperature (Absolute Zero).
Temperature measures average KE. At absolute zero (0 K), particle motion is minimum. The graph is a direct linear relation.
70. Relationship between motion of particles and temperature.
Higher temperature means faster particle motion. Lower temperature means slower motion.
71. Justify the need for fixed points in calibration.
Fixed points provide standard reference temperatures: Ice point (0°C) and Steam point (100°C).
72. How can a catalyst increase the rate of reaction?
A catalyst lowers activation energy, providing an easier path for the reaction, increasing the rate.
73. Describe structure and function of liquid-in-glass thermometer.
Consists of a glass tube with a bulb containing mercury/alcohol. Liquid expands/contracts to indicate temperature.
74. Can we increase internal energy without increasing temperature? Why is water not used in thermometers?
Yes, during phase change internal energy increases but temperature stays constant. Water is not used due to irregular expansion and sticking to glass.
75. Describe forces between magnetic poles.
Like poles repel; unlike poles attract. Magnets attract magnetic materials like iron.
76. Describe induced magnetism.
Occurs when a magnetic material becomes a magnet when placed in a magnetic field. It is usually temporary.
77. Draw pattern and direction of magnetic field lines around a bar magnet.
Lines emerge from North and enter South outside. Inside, they go from South to North.
78. Difference between magnetic and non-magnetic materials.
Magnetic (iron, nickel) are attracted by magnets. Non-magnetic (wood, plastic) are not.
79. Differentiate between temporary, permanent, and electromagnets.
Temporary magnets lose magnetism quickly. Permanent retain it. Electromagnets require electric current.
80. Factors affecting strength of electromagnet.
Number of turns of coil, amount of current, and type of core (soft iron).
81. What is neutral zone?
A region where magnetic fields cancel each other, so the net magnetic field is zero.
82. Is there any material with no magnetic behavior?
No. All materials show some magnetic effect, but in many it is extremely weak (diamagnetic).
83. What is magnetic field?
The region around a magnet where magnetic force is felt. Lines go North to South.
84. State that soft magnetic materials provide shielding.
Soft iron can redirect magnetic field lines, reducing the effect inside a region (magnetic shielding).
85. Analyze applications of magnets in recording technology.
Used in hard drives and tapes to store data by magnetizing small regions.
86. Describe the nature of Earth's magnetic field.
Earth acts like a giant magnet. It protects from cosmic radiation and helps in navigation.
87. Uses of permanent magnets and electromagnets in daily life.
Permanent: speakers, compasses. Electromagnets: cranes, electric bells, motors.
88. State direction of magnetic field at a point.
The direction in which a North pole would move at that point.
89. Define hypothesis, theory, and law.
Hypothesis: testable idea. Theory: well-tested explanation. Law: proven statement of fact.

Science: study of nature. Technology: application of science. Engineering: designing and building solutions.

92. What is Error? Explain its types. Also identifying sources of systematic and random error in measurements and suggesting steps to correct them. 5 Marks
An error is the difference between the measured value and the actual (true) value of a physical quantity. It is not necessarily a "mistake" but an uncertainty in measurement.

Random Error: Occurs when repeated measurements of the same quantity give different values under the same conditions.
Sources: Fluctuations in environment (temperature, voltage) or slight variations in the observer's technique.
Correction: Take multiple readings and calculate the mean (average) value to minimize the effect.

Systematic Error: Occurs when measurements are consistently shifted in one direction (always too high or always too low).
Sources: Zero error in instruments (e.g., a weighing scale not at zero), poorly calibrated equipment, or faulty experimental setup.
Correction: Apply a zero correction (mathematical adjustment) or recalibrate the instrument against a standard.

Motion Type Distance-Time Graph (s-t) Speed-Time Graph (v-t)

94. Analyze the Distance travelled in speed vs time graphs [by determining the area under the graph for cases of motion with constant speed or constant acceleration]. 5 Marks
In a speed-time (v-t) graph, the area under the line numerically represents the total distance traveled by the object.

Constant Speed: The area formed is a rectangle.
Distance = Area of Rectangle = Speed × Time

Constant Acceleration (from rest): The area formed is a triangle.
Distance = Area of Triangle = ½ × base × height = ½ × t × v

General Case (Trapezium): For an object with initial velocity u and final velocity v:
Distance = ½(u + v) × t
95. Justify how the gradient of the speed vs time graph gives the acceleration. 5 Marks
Acceleration is defined as the rate of change of velocity per unit time (a = Δv/Δt).

On a speed-time graph:
• The Vertical change (y-axis) represents the change in velocity (Δv).
• The Horizontal change (x-axis) represents the change in time (Δt).
• The Gradient (Slope) of a line is defined as Rise/Run.

Substituting the physical quantities:
Gradient = Change in Speed / Time Interval = Δv/Δt

Since Δv/Δt = a, the gradient of a speed-time graph is equal to the acceleration.
96. Write a Complete note on Gravitational Field strength. 5 Marks
Gravitational Field Strength (g) is the gravitational force acting per unit mass at a specific point in space.

Formula: g = W/m, where W is the weight (force) and m is the mass.
Unit: Newtons per kilogram (N/kg) or meters per second squared (m/s²).
On Earth: Near the surface, the value is approximately 9.8 N/kg.
Nature: It is a vector quantity directed toward the center of the planet or mass.
Variation: It decreases as the distance from the center of the planet increases. It is also weaker on the Moon (approx. 1.6 N/kg) compared to Earth because the Moon has less mass.
97. What is equilibrium? Explain its types also state its conditions. (examples/mathematical relation). 5 Marks
A body is in equilibrium if it is at rest or moving with uniform velocity (resultant force and torque are zero).

Conditions:
First Condition: ΣF = 0 (The sum of all forces is zero).
Second Condition: Στ = 0 (The sum of all torques/moments is zero).

Types:
• Stable: Returns to its original position after a slight tilt (e.g., a book flat on a table).
• Unstable: Moves further away from its original position when tilted (e.g., a pencil standing on its tip).
• Neutral: Stays in its new position when moved (e.g., a ball rolling on a flat floor).
98. What is terminal velocity? On what factors does the drag force acting on a falling body depends? Explain. 5 Marks
Terminal velocity is the constant maximum velocity reached by a falling object when the downward force of gravity is exactly balanced by the upward drag force (Fnet = 0).

Factors affecting Drag Force:
• Speed: As the speed of the object increases, the drag force increases.
• Surface Area: Objects with a larger surface area (like an open parachute) experience more drag.
• Shape: Aerodynamic or streamlined shapes reduce drag.
• Fluid Density: Drag is higher in denser fluids (e.g., water vs. air).
99. What is centripetal force? And also identify the sources of centripetal force in real life examples. 5 Marks
Centripetal force is the force that acts on a body moving in a circular path and is directed toward the center around which the body is moving.

Real-Life Examples and Sources:
• Stone on a String: The source is the Tension in the string.
• Moon orbiting Earth: The source is the Gravitational Force from the Earth.
• Car turning a corner: The source is the Friction between the tires and the road.
• Electron orbiting Nucleus: The source is the Electrostatic Force.
100. Define and apply Pascal's law. 5 Marks
Pascal's Law states that pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted undiminished to every portion of the fluid and to the walls of the container.

Applications:
• Hydraulic Press: A small force on a small piston creates a massive force on a large piston to crush objects or lift heavy weights.
• Hydraulic Brakes: When a driver presses the brake pedal, the pressure is transmitted through the brake fluid to all wheels simultaneously to stop the vehicle.
101. How are hydraulic lifts capable of lifting heavy loads. Derive a mathematical formula. 5 Marks
Hydraulic lifts use the principle that pressure is constant throughout an enclosed liquid (P₁ = P₂). By applying a small force over a small area, a much larger force is generated over a larger area.

Derivation:
Let F₁ be the force on the small piston of area A₁, and F₂ be the force on the large piston of area A₂.
According to Pascal's Law:
P₁ = P₂
F₁/A₁ = F₂/A₂
Rearranging for F₂:
F₂ = F₁ × (A₂/A₁)
Since A₂ > A₁, the output force F₂ is multiplied, allowing the lift to move heavy loads.
102. Prove that Kinetic Energy Eₖ = ½mv² [proof through kinematic graphs]. 5 Marks
Consider an object of mass m at rest (u=0) accelerated to a velocity v by a force F over a distance s.

Work Done (W): Work done equals the Kinetic Energy gained.
Eₖ = W = F × s

From Newton's Second Law: F = ma.

From a Speed-Time Graph:
The distance s is the area under the triangle: s = ½ × base × height = ½ × t × v.
The acceleration a is the gradient: a = v/t.

Substitution:
Eₖ = (ma) × s
Eₖ = m × (v/t) × (½vt)
The t values cancel out:
Eₖ = ½ m × v × v = ½mv²

Hence Proved.
103. Prove and use the formula for Gravitational potential energy. 5 Marks
Gravitational Potential Energy (GPE) is the energy possessed by a body due to its position in a gravitational field.

Derivation:
Consider an object of mass m being lifted vertically through a height h at a constant speed.
To lift the object, a force F equal to its weight (W) must be applied: F = mg.
Work done (W) in lifting the object is given by: W = Force × Displacement.
Substituting the values: W = (mg) × h.
Since the work done is stored as potential energy:
GPE = mgh

Use: This formula is used to calculate energy in hydropower dams, falling objects, and roller coasters. For example, a 2 kg book on a 1.5 m shelf has GPE = 2 × 9.8 × 1.5 = 29.4 Joules.
104. Show that liquid in a container exerts pressure equal to P = ρgh. What is the effect of depth on pressure of liquid. 5 Marks
Derivation:
Consider a cylinder of liquid with cross-sectional area A, height h, and density ρ.
The volume of liquid is V = A × h.
The mass of liquid is m = density × volume = ρ × A × h.
The weight (F) of the liquid column is W = mg = ρAhg.
Pressure (P) is defined as force per unit area: P = F/A.
Substituting the force: P = ρAhg/A.
P = ρgh

Effect of Depth:
Liquid pressure is directly proportional to depth (P ∝ h). As depth increases, the weight of the liquid column above increases, thus increasing the pressure. This is why dam walls are thicker at the bottom.
105. Justify and illustrate how to determine the density of a substance [Including liquid, regular solid, and irregular solid]. 5 Marks
Density is mass per unit volume (ρ = m/V).

Regular Solid: Measure mass using a balance. Measure dimensions (length, width, height) with a ruler/vernier caliper. V = l × w × h. Calculate ρ = m/V.

Liquid: Measure the mass of an empty graduated cylinder (m₁). Add liquid and measure new mass (m₂). Mass of liquid m = m₂ - m₁. Note the volume (V) directly from the cylinder. Calculate ρ = m/V.

Irregular Solid (Displacement Method):
• Measure the mass (m) of the solid using a balance.
• Fill a displacement (Eureka) can with water up to the spout.
• Submerge the solid; the displaced water is collected in a measuring cylinder.
• The volume of displaced water = Volume of the solid (V).
• Calculate ρ = m/V.
106. Describe, qualitatively, the particle structure of solids, liquids and gases. 5 Marks
The properties of matter depend on the arrangement and motion of their particles:

Solids: Particles are closely packed in a fixed, regular lattice. Strong intermolecular forces hold them together. They only vibrate about fixed positions. Solids have a fixed shape and volume.

Liquids: Particles are close together but in a random arrangement. Forces are strong enough to keep volume fixed but weak enough to allow particles to slide over each other. They take the shape of the container.

Gases: Particles are very far apart with negligible forces between them. They move randomly at high speeds in all directions. Gases have no fixed shape or volume and are highly compressible.
107. Describe the relationship between the motion of particles and temperature [Including absolute zero]. 5 Marks
Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy (KE) of the particles in a substance.

Direct Relation: As temperature increases, particles move faster (higher KE). In gases, this results in higher pressure; in solids, more vigorous vibrations.

Absolute Zero: The lowest possible temperature is -273.15°C (or 0 K). At this point, particle motion reaches its theoretical minimum (least possible KE).

Kelvin Scale: The Kelvin scale starts at absolute zero, meaning T (K) = Temp (°C) + 273.
108. Explain, with examples, how a physical property which varies with temperature may be used for the measurement of temperature. 5 Marks
Any physical property that changes continuously and linearly with temperature is called a thermometric property.

Examples:
• Expansion of Liquid: In mercury/alcohol thermometers, the volume of the liquid increases with temperature.
• Electrical Resistance: In resistance thermometers (RTDs), the electrical resistance of a metal wire increases as it gets hotter.
• Voltage (EMF): In thermocouples, a temperature difference between two junctions of different metals produces a measurable voltage.
• Pressure of Gas: In constant-volume gas thermometers, the pressure of a fixed mass of gas increases with temperature.
109. Describe the construction and working of different types of gas thermometers. 5 Marks
Gas thermometers are highly accurate and used as standards for calibrating other thermometers.

Constant Volume Gas Thermometer: A bulb filled with gas is connected to a manometer. As temperature changes, gas pressure changes. The mercury level is adjusted to keep the gas volume constant. Temperature is calculated using P ∝ T.

Constant Pressure Gas Thermometer: The pressure is kept constant while the volume of the gas expands or contracts with temperature changes. V ∝ T (Charles's Law).

Working: They utilize the ideal gas laws. Because gases expand significantly and uniformly, these thermometers are sensitive to very small temperature changes.
110. Analyze how structure of liquid in glass thermometer can be modified to improve its performance. 5 Marks
Sensitivity: Use a thinner capillary tube (narrower bore) and a larger bulb. A small expansion will result in a larger movement of the liquid thread.

Responsiveness (Quickness): Use a bulb with a thin glass wall and a liquid that is a good conductor (like mercury) to allow heat to reach the liquid faster.

Range: Use a longer stem or a thermometric liquid with a very high boiling point and very low freezing point (like alcohol for cold climates).

Linearity: Ensure the bore of the capillary is uniform throughout its length.
111. Explain any two states of matter. Giving complete information about their particle structures with diagrams? 5 Marks
(Choosing Solid and Gas for contrast)

Solid State:
Structure: Particles are arranged in a tight, 3D regular pattern called a lattice.
Forces: Very strong attractive forces.
Motion: Only vibration around fixed points; no translational motion.

Gaseous State:
Structure: Particles are very far apart (large empty spaces).
Forces: Almost zero intermolecular forces.
Motion: High-speed, random motion in all directions, colliding with each other and container walls.
112. Write working of thermocouple thermometers. 5 Marks
A thermocouple consists of two wires of different metals (e.g., copper and iron) joined at two junctions.

Principle: It works on the Seebeck Effect.

Working: One junction (reference) is kept at a constant cold temperature (like ice), while the other (probe) is placed in the substance to be measured.

Voltage Generation: The temperature difference between the junctions creates an electromotive force (EMF) or voltage.

Measurement: This voltage is measured by a millivoltmeter. Since voltage is proportional to the temperature difference, the temperature can be determined.

Advantage: It can measure very high temperatures and rapid changes because of its low heat capacity.
113. What is meant by the sensitivity, range and linearity of thermometers? Explain each in detail. 5 Marks
Sensitivity: The change in the thermometric property per unit change in temperature. A sensitive thermometer shows a large change in reading for a very small change in temperature.

Range: The interval between the minimum and maximum temperatures a thermometer can measure (e.g., a clinical thermometer has a range of 35°C to 42°C).

Linearity: A thermometer is linear if the thermometric property changes at a constant rate with temperature. On a graph of property vs. temperature, it should be a straight line. This allows for an evenly spaced scale.
114. Explain qualitatively in terms of the domain theory of magnetism how materials can be magnetized and demagnetized. 5 Marks
Domain Theory: Magnetic materials contain small regions called "domains" that act like tiny magnets. In unmagnetized iron, these domains point in random directions, canceling each other out.

Magnetization:
• Stroking: Moving a magnet along a bar aligns the domains in one direction.
• Solenoid: Placing the material inside a coil with DC current uses the magnetic field to snap domains into alignment.

Demagnetization:
• Heating/Hammering: Provides energy to the particles, causing them to vibrate violently and fall back into random alignment.
• AC Solenoid: Using a solenoid with alternating current and slowly withdrawing the material randomizes the domains.
115. Differentiate between ferromagnetic, paramagnetic and diamagnetic materials. 5 Marks
Ferromagnetic: Have strongly aligned domains. They are strongly attracted by magnets and can be permanently magnetized (e.g., Iron, Nickel, Cobalt).

Paramagnetic: Have atoms with randomly oriented magnetic moments. They are weakly attracted by external fields and lose magnetism when the field is removed (e.g., Aluminum, Oxygen).

Diamagnetic: These materials create an induced magnetic field in the opposite direction to an external field. They are weakly repelled by magnets (e.g., Copper, Bismuth, Water).
116. Describe the nature of the Earth's magnetic field. 5 Marks
The Earth acts as if it has a giant bar magnet at its center.

Orientation: The Earth's magnetic North pole is near the geographic South pole. This is why the North pole of a compass needle points toward geographic North.

Protection: The magnetic field (magnetosphere) deflects harmful cosmic radiation and solar winds, preventing them from stripping away our atmosphere.

Navigation: Many migratory animals (birds, sea turtles) have internal biological compasses (biomagnetism) that allow them to sense the Earth's field lines to navigate thousands of miles.
117. Explain with examples that physics has many sub-fields, and involves interdisciplinary fields. 5 Marks
Physics is the fundamental science that studies matter and energy. Because it explains the "rules" of the universe, it overlaps with many other fields (Interdisciplinary).

Examples:
• Biophysics: Applying physics to biological systems (e.g., DNA structure).
• Geophysics: Physics of the Earth (e.g., earthquakes).
• Astrophysics: Physics of celestial bodies.

Fields of Physics:
Physics is an incredibly broad science that seeks to understand the fundamental laws of the universe. Here are ten of its key fields:

Mechanics: The study of the motion of physical objects and the forces that cause that motion.
Thermodynamics: The branch that deals with heat, temperature, and their relation to energy and work.
Electromagnetism: The study of electric and magnetic fields and the interactions between charged particles.
Optics: The field focused on the behavior and properties of light, including its interactions with matter.
Nuclear Physics: The study of the constituents and interactions of atomic nuclei.
Atomic Physics: The field that examines the structure of the atom and the arrangement of electrons around the nucleus.
Quantum Mechanics: The branch of physics dealing with physical phenomena at microscopic scales, where the action is on the order of the Planck constant.
Acoustics: The interdisciplinary science that deals with the study of mechanical waves in gases, liquids, and solids, including vibration, sound, ultrasound, and infrasound.
Relativity: The study of the relationship between space, time, and gravity, primarily based on Albert Einstein's theories.
Particle Physics: The branch that studies the nature of the particles that constitute matter and radiation.

Explanation:

Q.7 Which is more difficult to stop in 5 second and why? 1. A 2500kg car moving with v₁ =10 m s⁻¹ 2. A 5000 kg van moving with V₁ =4 m s⁻¹
Given:
Car: m₁ = 2500 kg, v₁ = 10 m/s
Van: m₂ = 5000 kg, v₂ = 4 m/s
Time to stop = 5 s (same for both)

Required:
Which has greater difficulty to stop and reason.

Solution:
Momentum (p) decides difficulty (larger Δp needs larger force for same time).
p_car = m₁v₁ = 2500 × 10 = 25,000 kg m/s
p_van = m₂v₂ = 5000 × 4 = 20,000 kg m/s
Since p_car > p_van, the car is more difficult to stop.
(Reason: Impulse = F × t = Δp; same t → larger Δp needs larger F.)

Explanation:
Newton's second law in momentum form shows that stopping depends on change in momentum. The car has higher momentum despite lower mass because of higher velocity. This is why heavier but slower objects can sometimes be easier to stop.

Acceleration:

Time t is not given, therefore numerical value of acceleration cannot be calculated.

Explanation:
For uniform acceleration, average velocity is simply the arithmetic mean of initial and final velocities. Acceleration requires time interval or distance; without either, only V_av can be found. This highlights the importance of complete data in kinematics problems.
